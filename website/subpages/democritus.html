Democritus

Democritus was an Ancient Greek pre-Socratic philosopher, most recognized for his development of the principles of atomic theory. He was born in around 460 BC in Abdera, Thrace, and died circa 370 BC (aged ~90). Known to many as the ‘laughing philosopher’ for his cheerfulness throughout his philosophies, he is considered a highly influential figure in establishing modern science. Although none of his supposed 73 works have been recovered in full, many fragments of his writing have survived, and were often cited by later philosophers such as Aristotle.

Contributions to Atomic Theory

Democritus developed the theory of atomism in collaboration with his mentor Leucippus, who was known to have influenced Democritus’s work, although little information exists on Leucippus himself. They believed that all matter is composed of miniscule, eternal particles, which vary in shape and size. They called these particles atomos (Greek for uncuttable/indivisible), a word which was later derived into the modern term ‘atom’. These indivisible particles exist in a void, an infinite vacuum which contains an infinite number of particles, thus making up the entire physical world. Democritus also believed that atoms were constantly moving and colliding with each other, and collisions of different atoms led to the creation of different objects and substances.
